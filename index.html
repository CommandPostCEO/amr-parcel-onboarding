    What Is TMS & Why It Matters

    Orientation — purpose, scope, and the RSM's role in TMS

    TMS Overview
    The Transportation Management System controls routing rules, carrier assignments, and shipment planning for the week ahead. For the IB Parcel RSM, the most critical TMS touchpoint is the Friday Settings — configuring routing for the following week before the Monday capacity request cycle begins.
    Foundation
    TMS vs. Dashboard Relationship
    The TMS is the instruction layer — it tells carriers what to expect and sets routing rules. The Parcel Dashboard and OTG/In Network reports are the visibility layer — showing you whether execution matches the plan. Mismatches between the two are your signal to escalate or adjust.
    Foundation
    RSM TMS Responsibilities
    The RSM owns: (1) Friday weekly setup for the coming week; (2) Mid-week adjustments for exceptions or volume changes; (3) Coordination with OEMs and carriers to ensure TMS settings reflect actual handover plans; (4) Validation that EDI 856 carrier codes are correctly mapped in the RRG.
    Foundation

    Friday TMS Settings — Step by Step

    The weekly configuration workflow every RSM must master

    Step 1 — Review Forecast vs. Actuals
    Before touching TMS, pull the Forecast vs. Actuals report and CPT Pivot. Understand the volume delta between what was planned and what actually moved. This informs your routing decisions for the following week.
    Friday Workflow
    Step 2 — Check OTG & In Network Status
    Before locking in next week's settings, confirm the current week's OTG volume disposition. Any volume rolling over from the current week (particularly UPS) affects your starting point for next week's settings.
    Friday Workflow
    Step 3 — Configure Carrier Assignments
    Set the carrier routing for each OEM/origin lane for the upcoming week. Key rules: (1) US — all 3 carriers possible; (2) CA — UPS primary, FedEx for AirTags; (3) MX — FedEx sole carrier, UPS does not service MX; (4) CL — DHL primary. Confirm no capacity constraints from Monday's UPS request that require rerouting.
    Friday Workflow
    Step 4 — Validate RRG Alignment
    Cross-reference TMS settings against the RRG Gateway Matrix and Parcel Account Numbers tabs. Ensure the carrier codes entered match the EDI 856 codes in the RRG. Mismatches here cause downstream visibility failures.
    Friday Workflow
    OEM-Specific Routing Confirmations
    Confirm TMS settings reflect actual OEM capabilities before locking in. Trust but verify — don't rely solely on TMS defaults.
    Friday Workflow
    Step 6 — Document & Hand Off
    Log the TMS settings snapshot before leaving for the weekend. Ensure any exceptions or non-standard configurations are noted in the daily handover for Monday visibility. If a synch call or coverage is in place over the weekend, communicate open items.
    Friday Workflow

    TMS & the UPS Monday Capacity Cycle

    How TMS settings feed into the weekly UPS capacity request

    The Friday → Monday Connection
    The TMS settings you lock in on Friday directly inform the UPS Monday capacity request. The capacity numbers you submit Monday morning are based on the routing volume you've configured in TMS for each origin/lane. If TMS is wrong, the capacity ask is wrong.
    Cycle
    UPS Automation Tool
    The UPS capacity automation tool reads from or feeds into TMS settings. Understand how these two systems interact — specifically, whether automation handles lane-level routing or whether manual overrides in TMS are required for exceptions.
    Cycle
    IB/OB Synch Impact
    The Monday UPS IB/OB Parcel Synch call may trigger TMS mid-week adjustments. If OB volumes shift significantly, the RSM may need to update TMS routing mid-week to rebalance capacity. Always check whether a synch call outcome requires a TMS update before the next Friday cycle.
    Cycle
    D

    TMS Exception Handling & Ad Hoc Adjustments

    When and how to make mid-week changes

    When to Adjust TMS Mid-Week
    Mid-week TMS changes are triggered by: (1) carrier capacity constraints identified post-Monday; (2) OEM equipment or lane constraints (e.g., V158, CGO); (3) NPI surge requiring rerouting; (4) Dead freight events; (5) Carrier escalations (e.g., FedEx pushback on ad hoc requests).
    Ad Hoc
    FedEx-Specific TMS Caution
    FedEx does not have dynamic EDDs. If a TMS change requires FedEx to adjust expected delivery dates, expect pushback and slow response times. Build extra lead time into any FedEx TMS adjustments vs. UPS.
    Ad Hoc
    RRG Version Control During Changes
    Any ad hoc TMS change that introduces a new lane or carrier code must be verified against the current RRG version. If the RRG doesn't reflect the change, initiate an RRG amendment as part of the ad hoc process.

    TMS Practice Exercise

    Hands-on validation before going independent

    Shadow a Full Friday TMS Setup
    Before going independent: shadow a full Friday TMS session end-to-end, including the Forecast vs. Actuals review, OTG check, carrier assignment configuration, RRG validation, and handover note. Ask questions at each step.
    Practice
    Own a Friday TMS Setup (Supervised)
    Run the Friday TMS setup independently with a senior RSM available for review and sign-off before the settings are confirmed. Repeat until confident.
    Practice
    Walk Through a Historical Exception
    Review a past mid-week TMS adjustment — ideally one triggered by a carrier escalation or capacity crunch. Understand what changed, why, and how it was resolved. Common examples: FedEx MX pushback on ad hoc requests; CL DHL volume reroute to FedEx backup.

    • Difficult to work with for ad hoc or out-of-plan requests
    • Best option for MX BAU and NPI
    • Toluca (MX) location well-managed — govt/military airport
    • Currently sole MX carrier — UPS does not service MX
    • Key contact: Ashleigh and her manager
